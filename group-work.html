My CSS Group Presentation Journey: Navigating Teamwork Through Tuckman's Stages

Tuckman's Stages of Group Development

When we first received the Kopano Enterprises CSS group presentation, it felt like one of those assignments that looks simple on paper but becomes very real once you start working on it. What made the experience even more meaningful was that we chose our own groups based on people we naturally got along with.

Looking back, our journey followed Tuckman's stages of group development almost perfectly, from the moment we formed the group to the moment we walked out of the boarding room after presenting.

1 Forming

Choosing Each Other & Appointing Our Leader

The forming stage was actually exciting. Since we were allowed to choose our group members, we went with people we vibed with — classmates we sit with, laugh with, or already understand academically.

That's also when we chose our group leader, Mellisa. We picked her because she's always active in class, quick to answer questions, and just naturally carries herself with leadership energy.

We created our WhatsApp group immediately, shared the assignment PDF, and started planning how we would approach the simulation and the presentation.

2 Storming

The Panic, The Small Clashes & The Reality Check

Like any real group, storming hit us when the assignment started feeling real. Some people were unsure about the company challenges, others had different ideas of how the meeting simulation should run.

  • Someone would panic because their slides were not done
  • Responses in the group chat delayed
  • Some members felt shy about presenting
  • A few of us struggled with the idea of acting out a "formal meeting"

It wasn't dramatic, but the small misunderstandings were definitely there. The good thing is — we kept pushing through.

3 Norming

Finding Our Rhythm & Preparing Properly

Eventually, things started aligning. We divided the work, assigned roles, and agreed on using PowerPoint as our main presentation tool. Everyone worked on their part, and we reviewed each other's slides to make sure the flow was professional and consistent.

Before school holidays, we met a few times to discuss the structure. And then after the holidays — with only three days left before submission day — we became very serious. We met again, practised our lines, rehearsed the meeting simulation, and fixed anything that still looked confusing.

This was the moment where the teamwork truly started showing. People who were shy began to open up, and those who were scared to speak in front of others made the effort to practice more.

4 Performing

Presentation Day in the Boarding Room

On presentation day, we agreed to wear formal attire just to look more professional and well-coordinated. And honestly? We looked the part.

We presented in the boarding room, just us and ma'am sitting alone at the back, observing everything. The environment made the whole thing feel even more serious.

Not everything was perfect:

  • Some members stuttered
  • A few got scared when questions came
  • We panicked during some of the Q&A from the lecturer
  • We couldn't answer every question smoothly

But even with all the nerves, we managed. Everyone spoke. Everyone delivered their section. The meeting simulation flowed, and our slides supported the presentation well. The important part is that we tried, and we worked together all the way until the end.

5 Adjourning

We Finished — And We Felt Proud

After we presented, there was this quiet moment of relief and pride. We congratulated each other, laughed about the parts where we panicked, and thanked the person who submitted the slides on Turnitin.

Even though the presentation wasn't perfect, it showed growth, teamwork, and commitment. We walked out knowing we did our best — and that's the true spirit behind Tuckman's final stage: closing the chapter together.

Key Learning: Success isn't about perfection; it's about growth, commitment, and working together through challenges.

Final Reflection

This group presentation experience taught me that teamwork isn't just about dividing slides. It's about communication, support, patience, and accepting that everyone has strengths and weaknesses. We didn't deliver a perfect corporate-style presentation, but for a group of first-year students learning as we go, we delivered something honest, hardworking, and truly collaborative.

"Most importantly, it showed me how real group dynamics work — and how each stage shapes the final outcome."

The journey through Tuckman's stages gave me valuable insights into how teams form, face challenges, and eventually succeed together. This experience has prepared me for future collaborative work in both academic and professional settings.

As I continue my studies in Business Intelligence and Data Analytics, I know that teamwork will be essential. This first group project has given me the confidence to approach future collaborations with understanding, patience, and the knowledge that every team goes through similar growth processes.
