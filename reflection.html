Reflective Blog Post: My First University Group Presentation Journey

Coming into university, I knew group work would be a big part of the experience, but I didn't really understand what that meant until our ICT101 group presentation for Kopano Enterprises. Our task was to act as a consulting team and create a 10-minute presentation solving a company's communication problems. Looking back, our group's journey was a perfect example of Tuckman's stages of group development: Forming, Storming, Norming, Performing, and Adjourning.

Tuckman's Stages in Action

1 Forming: The Nervous Beginning

Our group was formed mostly by our tutor based on our student IDs. In the beginning, it was a bit awkward. We didn't know each other, and our first meeting on Microsoft Teams was just a lot of silent smiles and "hellos." We were all polite but nervous, trying to figure out who was good at what.

We started by simply reading the assignment brief together and agreeing that we needed to meet again. It felt like we were all waiting for someone else to take the lead.

2 Storming: The Reality of Clashing Ideas

This stage hit us about a week in. We had decided on the main challenges for Kopano Enterprises, but when it came to the solutions, we had very different ideas. One member wanted very detailed, theoretical solutions, while another thought we should keep it simple and practical.

A few of us were quieter and just agreed to avoid conflict. For a couple of days, our group chat went quiet because it felt like we weren't getting anywhere. This was the most challenging part. We resolved it when one member finally suggested we vote on the top three solutions to focus on. It wasn't a perfect fix, but it helped us move forward without anyone feeling completely ignored.

3 Norming: Finding Our Rhythm

After we got through the "storming," things started to click. We properly assigned roles based on our strengths. I volunteered to work on the visual aids because I'm comfortable with Canva. Another member who was a confident speaker took the lead on the introduction slides.

We created a shared PowerPoint online and set small, achievable deadlines for each other. For example, "Have slides 4-8 done by Friday." Having this structure made everything feel more manageable and fair. We started using a proper agenda for our meetings, which made them much shorter and more productive.

4 Performing: The Final Push and Presentation Day

In the final week before the presentation, we finally hit our stride. We practiced together over Teams twice. We gave each other feedback on our speaking parts, like "speak a bit slower" or "that slide needs a better image." We timed ourselves to make sure we didn't go over 10 minutes.

On the actual presentation day, we were all nervous, but because we had practiced and everyone knew their part, it went smoothly. We supported each other with nods, and when one person forgot a small point, the next person smoothly covered for them. It felt like we were a real team.

5 Adjourning: A Bittersweet End

After we presented and submitted the PDF, there was a sense of relief and accomplishment. We sent messages in the group chat like "Great job, everyone!" and "We did it!". However, since the module is still ongoing, we haven't officially "disbanded." The chat has gone quiet again, but now it feels more like a successful completion than the tense silence during the storming stage.

What I Learned About Communication and Teamwork

This experience taught me that conflict in a group is normal and can actually lead to a better final product if handled respectfully. I learned that clear communication isn't just about talking; it's about listening and being willing to compromise.

Key Insight: I also saw how important it is to have a clear structure, like an agenda and assigned roles, to keep everyone on track. While it was stressful at times, working in this group helped me build confidence and showed me that I can collaborate with different kinds of people to achieve a common goal.

It was a valuable first step in preparing for the professional world. This group project gave me practical experience in teamwork that I know will be essential throughout my university journey and future career in data analytics.

"This first group project taught me more about real-world collaboration than any textbook could. The challenges we faced and overcame together were invaluable learning experiences."
